Strong Password Creation Tips

General Tips for Strong Passwords

  • Length: Make your password at least 12-16 characters long. The longer, the better.
  • Complexity: Use a mix of uppercase letters, lowercase letters, numbers, and special characters.
  • Avoid Common Words: Don’t use dictionary words or common phrases. These are easily guessable.
  • Avoid Personal Information: Don’t use your name, birth date, or other easily discoverable personal information in your password.
  • Use Unpredictable Combinations: Randomize your passwords rather than using predictable patterns.
  • Use a Passphrase: A passphrase is a string of random words. For example, PurpleElephant$42Balloon!.
  • Unique Passwords for Different Accounts: Don’t use the same password across multiple accounts. Each account should have its own unique password.
  • Password Manager: Use a password manager to generate and store complex passwords securely.

Additional Tips for Even Stronger Passwords

  • Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA): Always enable 2FA where possible. It adds an extra layer of security beyond your password.
  • Use a Password Manager: Password managers generate and store strong, unique passwords for every site. This makes managing complex passwords easier.
  • Update Passwords Regularly: Consider changing your passwords every few months, especially for sensitive accounts.
  • Check for Leaked Passwords: Use tools like Have I Been Pwned to check if your passwords have been involved in a data breach.
  • Be Cautious with Security Questions: Don't use easily guessable answers to security questions, like your mother’s maiden name or your pet’s name.
  • Avoid Public Wi-Fi for Sensitive Logins: Avoid logging into sensitive accounts over public Wi-Fi. If necessary, use a VPN for added security.
  • Store Passwords Securely: If you're manually storing passwords, ensure they're encrypted. Password managers handle this for you.
  • Don’t Share Passwords: Never share your password with anyone. If you need to share access to an account, use features that allow shared access without giving out your password.

Practical Examples and Guidelines

  • Use Randomness: Create passwords that don’t follow predictable patterns. For example, T!m3L@mp#9 is better than football123.
  • Don’t Use Common Substitutions: Replacing “a” with “@” or “s” with “$” may seem clever, but these substitutions are commonly known and can be guessed easily.
  • Be Careful with Password Hints: Avoid creating password hints that can give away your password. They should be obscure and not related to your password.
  • Use Multiple Random Words: Combine unrelated random words with numbers and symbols. For example, Moon^Hedge8Car!3 is strong because it’s random and long.
  • Check the Strength of Your Password: Use online tools to check how strong your password is, such as password strength checkers provided by password managers.

Important Notes on Common Password Pitfalls

  • Avoid Keyboard Patterns: Don’t use simple keyboard patterns like qwerty or asdfgh.
  • Don’t Reuse Passwords: If one account is compromised, all other accounts with the same password are at risk.
  • Use a Different Password for Critical Accounts: Sensitive accounts like email, banking, and social media should have extra-strong, unique passwords.
  • Don’t Use Personal Data: Don’t use your name, email address, or other easily available personal information as part of your password.
  • Beware of Phishing Attacks: Even with a strong password, be cautious of phishing emails that may trick you into giving out your login credentials.
